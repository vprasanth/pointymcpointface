ACK — A Lightweight Human Acknowledgement Protocol

Status:
Informational
Category:
Best Current Practice
Updates:
None
Intended Audience:
Humans working in Slack

Abstract

This document specifies ACK, a lightweight protocol for acknowledging helpful actions performed by humans in collaborative systems. ACK is designed to be fast, low-overhead, and compatible with existing Slack-based workflows.

ACK enables participants to explicitly acknowledge assistance, effort, or positive contributions using a minimal syntax, without introducing ceremony, governance, or additional process.

1. Introduction

In distributed systems, acknowledgements (ACKs) are used to confirm receipt, signal progress, and ensure shared understanding.

In human systems, similar signals are often implicit, delayed, or missing entirely.

The ACK protocol addresses this gap by providing a simple, explicit mechanism for human acknowledgement that is:

  • Fast
  • Visible
  • Low friction
  • Socially lightweight

2. Terminology

The key words MUST, SHOULD, and MAY are to be interpreted as described in RFC 2119.

Participant: A human user within a Slack workspace.

ACK: An acknowledgement signal indicating appreciation or recognition.

Emitter: The participant issuing an ACK.

Recipient: The participant receiving an ACK.

3. Protocol Overview

ACK operates within Slack message streams.

An ACK is emitted by appending the token ++ to a participant mention.

Example:

@alice++

This indicates that the emitter acknowledges the recipient for a contribution, assistance, or action.

4. Design Goals

ACK is intentionally minimal.

The protocol is designed to:

  • Avoid interrupting existing workflows
  • Require no additional UI interaction
  • Be human-readable
  • Be socially safe to use frequently

ACK MUST NOT require:

  • Approval flows
  • Scoring justification
  • Managerial oversight
  • Retrospective explanation

5. Usage Semantics

ACKs MAY be used to acknowledge:

  • Answering a question
  • Unblocking a teammate
  • Shipping a fix
  • Reviewing work
  • Being reliable
  • Doing the right thing quietly

ACKs SHOULD be sent close in time to the acknowledged action.

ACKs MAY be public and are intentionally visible to the channel.

6. Observability

ACK implementations MAY provide lightweight aggregation or summaries (e.g. leaderboards or counts).

Such features SHOULD remain secondary and MUST NOT interfere with the primary function of acknowledgement.

ACK is not a performance evaluation system.

7. Security Considerations

ACK is not security-sensitive.

However, implementations SHOULD avoid:

  • Incentive distortion
  • Competitive misuse
  • Over-optimization of acknowledgement signals

ACK is designed to reinforce healthy team dynamics, not replace trust.

8. Philosophy

In networking, ACKs confirm progress.

In teams, they do too.

ACK exists to make small moments of appreciation explicit, visible, and easy — because reliable systems depend on reliable humans.

9. Conclusion

ACK provides a simple protocol for human acknowledgement in collaborative environments.

It does not attempt to measure value.

It merely confirms that value was noticed.
